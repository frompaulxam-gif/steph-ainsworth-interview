JAMES: So I'm talking to Dr. Steph Ainsworth today. Hi Steph, nice to see you.
STEPH: Hi James.
JAMES: Can you tell us about your project in a nutshell?
STEPH: Yes, so it's a project exploring teacher resilience. We were interested in: what does it really mean to be a resilient teacher? And we decided to adopt a socio-ecological framing, which means that when we're thinking about resilience, it's not something that just sits in the individual, like an individual trait. It's more of a complex process that happens across systems.
JAMES: So tell us a bit more about that. When you say across systems, what does that look like?
STEPH: So in our data, what we found was that when we talked to teachers about what affects your resilience, we came up with this 5S model, because the things that they were saying we could map across five Ss, which are self, school, social, system, and society. So in terms of self, it was about the things that they did as an individual to look after themselves. School was things like workload, leadership, et cetera. Social — lots of stuff came up in the data about teachers being able to draw upon social support from colleagues, but also about social relationships they have with parents, which could be a risk factor or a protective factor. And then system is about things like curriculum, policies in the broadest sense. And then finally, stuff came up about society, which was interesting — around it really affecting their resilience in terms of how they feel perceived by the general public. And often they feel that there's not a very positive kind of perception of teachers, and they're often kind of being hit by a stick, really, rather than thanked.
JAMES: Okay, so what motivated you to look so deeply at teacher resilience?
STEPH: So I think mainly the fact that I used to be a teacher myself, so I was a primary school teacher for a few years, and it was such a rewarding job. I think it's one of the most important jobs in the world, but I was also very aware of how challenging it was. And so when I came into this university environment and got a chance to do some research, it was one of the first things that I wanted to do, because I was interested in, you know, what do we know theoretically about this? Like, how can we understand better these challenges? And really importantly, what can we do about it, and how can we support teachers better?
JAMES: Okay, lovely. So what do you think were the two key findings, or two or three key findings, that you found out from your research?
STEPH: So one thing that I found interesting about the survey data — part of the project was that we did a survey with about 3,000 teachers, and that was designed to find out which are the most important factors for resilience, because there's lots of stuff out there already in the research about, we know these things do have some kind of relationship to resilience, like workload, for example. But we wanted to know, if you try and quantitatively rank the importance, which ones are the kind of key players. So we did that with a mixture of individual-level factors, like self-care, emotional intelligence, et cetera, and also school-level ones, like culture, workload. And when we ranked them, we found that the importance for resilience depended on which outcome you were looking at. So the things that were important for well-being weren't exactly the same as the things that were important for job satisfaction. And what we found was that for well-being, the individual-level factors overall are more important. So things like self-care, emotional intelligence — in terms of kind of being aware of emotions and using strategies to regulate them — those kind of things came out as being particularly associated with well-being. But the things for job satisfaction and also risk of burnout were more about the environment. So they were more about the workload, the culture, et cetera. And the reason I think that's important is because there's quite a strong kind of discourse, conversation, around teacher well-being in schools. People often talk about well-being policies, well-being initiatives, et cetera. But really, we need to think about resilience more broadly — that actually you could be really well because you go for a run every day, and you do all those things to look after yourself, and you do CBT strategies to keep positive thinking, but that's not necessarily going to make you enjoy your job more. Actually, if the school culture's toxic and your workload's excessive, you're probably going to be well somewhere else and leave your job. So we need this broader conception of teacher resilience. And to sort of strengthen all those outcomes, we need that mixture of individual coping strategies and environmental changes.
JAMES: Okay, so that's really relevant for an individual, but also for a school.
STEPH: Yeah, there's implications for both.
JAMES: And is there anything that you found particularly surprising or interesting from your findings?
STEPH: I think one of the things that surprised me, having been out of school for a few years since I've been at university — what seemed to have really changed is the influence of relationships with parents. So when I was working in primary, obviously parent partnerships were really important and we did a lot of work around that. But it came up as a much stronger risk factor than I was expecting in the schools that we went in to speak to. Because as well as the survey, we worked closely with eight project partner schools over a period of time. And when we talked to teachers and leaders there, they were saying that obviously they had some really good relationships with lots of parents, but that there was a sort of significant minority of parents where those relationships have become really problematic. There were lots of tensions there. And I think the general consensus was that that had become amplified since COVID, and there's been a shift in expectations between parents and teachers. So I think that's something I'd like to find out more about for future research, because it's quite a tricky problem to address because it's complex.
JAMES: And was there anything particularly you thought it would be really useful for teachers or school leaders to know — something they could actually do?
STEPH: Yeah. So I think for teachers, first of all, I think it's really important that they're able to see resilience for what it is, in the sense that it isn't an individual trait. So it's not about, oh, this person's more resilient than this person, which can sometimes make people feel like it's their fault if they're struggling. And kind of related to that, it's understanding that if you are struggling, you're definitely not on your own. Because one of the things that came up with the data — we did them in group interviews in their project partner schools — and quite often teachers would say to the person next to them, "I didn't realize you were feeling like that. I didn't know, you know, I thought it was just me," those kind of things. So there was something quite powerful just about the conversations, aside from trying to fix the problems. So it's truly important to talk to other people about how you feel, because just understanding it and getting it off your chest can really help. And also, yeah, by understanding the system as a whole, you can think about what kind of resources there are across the system that can help you. You know, you don't have to deal with all these challenges on your own. So the toolkit that we've created is freely available online, and you can dip into different aspects of the system as a teacher or student teacher and think about, you know — so if you're struggling with managing workload, there's kind of things on there that you might find useful, that kind of thing.
JAMES: I think that's really good. I mean, it sounds so useful, the toolkit, to have something really practical that is implementable. Have you seen it in action?
STEPH: Not yet, because we've only just developed it and we're still adding some more content at the moment. So we released a kind of beta version of it in March, where some modules are ready, but we're about to kind of go into a phase of getting some initial feedback from people who might have used it. But at the same time we're trying to develop other layers of the toolkit, so we're doing it in a bit of a phased way. And I'm hoping it'll be used in teacher education as well, even to support student teachers, so there's a lot going on. So it's still early days, but yeah, if anybody does use it and wants to get in touch about how they've used it, we'd love to hear about it. And yeah, if anyone's got any questions or anything — you're watching this — please do get in touch.
JAMES: Brilliant, thanks ever so much, Steph, really good.
STEPH: Thank you.
